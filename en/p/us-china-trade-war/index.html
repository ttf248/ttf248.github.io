Let’s start with some tangential points – the differences between Chinese-style socialism and capitalism, from the perspective of older generations who always said you need to build roads before getting rich. China’s infrastructure development – these things are all funded by the state, whereas in a capitalist society, they would be contracted out. In remote areas, there’s no profit motive, so companies wouldn’t willingly take on those projects. We’ve gone off-topic a bit, and many people might think trade wars don’t significantly impact their daily lives. However, China’s high-end manufacturing has always been relatively weak. As someone working in the IT industry, I can tell you that the core configurations of computers – memory, hard drives, CPUs, graphics cards – come from factories abroad. These components account for 50% of the total cost of a finished computer, and it’s clear that high-end manufacturing is essential. The collision between China and the United States is inevitable.

Wikipedia

2018-2020 China–United States trade war, often referred to as the “China–United States trade war” (English: China–United States trade war), or simply “US–China trade dispute,” “US–China trade friction,” “US–China trade conflict,” or “trade war” between the People’s Republic of China and the United States.

The trade dispute originated when U.S. President Donald Trump signed a memorandum on March 22, 2018, claiming that “China was stealing American intellectual property and trade secrets,” and invoking Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974 to impose tariffs on goods imported from China, totaling an estimated $600 billion. On July 6, 2018, the U.S. imposed additional tariffs of 25% on $340 billion worth of Chinese goods shipped to the United States.

Wikipedia

The United States and China had reached a consensus in May 2018 to temporarily suspend the trade war, and issued a joint statement seeking reconciliation. However, the Office of the U.S. Trade Representative announced its first list of tariffs on Chinese goods shipped to the US on June 16th, raising the existing 10% tariff rate to 25%. The China Tariff Committee subsequently retaliated with equivalent measures, and the Ministry of Commerce of China also restarted anti-dumping investigations into various U.S.-bound products. On July 6th, the Trump administration formally imposed a 25% customs duty on $34 billion worth of Chinese goods shipped to the US from the first list of tariffs, marking the formal implementation of Trump’s trade policy towards China (the remaining $16 billion in goods were subsequently subject to a 25% tariff on August 23rd). The Ministry of Commerce of China stated afterward that “The United States has violated WTO rules and launched the largest trade war in economic history.” According to the General Administration of Customs of China, the Chinese side immediately implemented counter-measures following the implementation of US tariffs.

On December 1st, during the G20 Buenos Aires Summit, leaders Xi Jinping and Donald Trump reached an agreement to hold a 90-day negotiation and suspend new trade measures during that period. After the deadline of March 1st, 2019 expired, the US side announced significant progress and extended the suspension of new trade measures.

Wikipedia

May 5, 2019, U.S. President Donald Trump announced the imposition of a 25% tariff on approximately $200 billion in Chinese goods, totaling $250 billion, as part of a trade war. The measure took effect on June 1st. On May 13th, China’s State Council Tariff Commission announced that starting June 1st, tariffs ranging from 5% to 25% would be levied on select imported goods originating in the United States valued at $60 billion. On June 1st, the U.S. Trade Representative’s Office announced a postponement of the imposition of a 25% tariff on U.S. products until June 15th. China’s State Council Tariff Commission stated that China’s retaliatory tariffs would take effect as scheduled on June 1st.

June 29th, leaders Xi Jinping and Donald Trump held talks at the G20 Osaka Summit, agreeing to resume economic negotiations. The United States agreed not to impose new tariffs on Chinese products.

Wikipedia

On August 1st, due to the Trump administration’s dissatisfaction with China’s purchasing process of American agricultural products, Trump announced on Twitter that starting September 1st, 2019, tariffs of 10% would be imposed on all US goods imported from China worth $300 billion. On August 5th, the Renminbi exchange rate against the dollar fell below the 7-yuan mark. The same day, the U.S. Treasury Department announced that it was designating China as a currency manipulator. Subsequently, the Chinese government announced a suspension of purchases of American agricultural products and on August 24th announced tariffs of 10% or 5% on approximately $750 billion worth of US goods, and restored tariffs on U.S. automobiles and their parts; while the U.S. increased the tariff rate on its $300 billion Chinese goods to 15%, and the current $250 billion Chinese goods to 25% as a countermeasure, but it was subsequently shelved.

On January 16th, 2020, China and the United States signed a Phase One trade agreement.
