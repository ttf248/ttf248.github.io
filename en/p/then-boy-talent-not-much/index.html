One, Childhood

Moving downtown, encountering computer books? Hacker resources? Becoming familiar with Windows systems? Those are later stories

My brother’s uncle runs a shop in the computer market We started using computers early on, so we developed a basic understanding of them. Later, in school and during computer classes, I remained quite interested. I heard about the computer competitions in junior high, thought they were really cool, but then after transferring schools, I put that idea aside During my first year of junior high, I was familiar with basic computer operations and often stood out in computer class If you’re familiar with it – yes, you read that right, not proficient, but familiar – with Microsoft Office suite, that would be even better

Moving

Regarding the move, once I got into the city, I ended up frequenting the library due to my neighbors. While I read a lot of novels, I also looked at many magazines. Computer News, Popular Software Increasingly interested in computers, with childhood admiration for hackers and actively learning related knowledge at school Understanding the basics of operating systems: Control Panel, CMD commands, VBS scripts

This magazine is quite suitable for beginners; it explains operations systematically using case studies software reviews, industry news, and more

High school

In my second year of high school, [this competition], the school leaders also paid it considerable attention There’s also a foundational hardware aspect – an alumnus donated a building to the university, including a new library and computer lab. It’s all so coincidental. With Bo’s explanations, he would be considered a computer expert in the class

Academic whiz + computer expert, knows how to hack computers and disable classroom surveillance software

The competition, despite its challenges, reached the finals. While I didn’t fully grasp everything, the questions focused on basic algorithms, but I did gain some valuable insights.

It’s still just half a bucket, like taking a trip

Four. College

When it came time to choose a major in college, I followed my family’s suggestion and chose automation. I actually wanted to study high-voltage electricity, hoping to work for the power company after graduation, but I didn’t learn much of the coursework. Self-directed learning is scarce in professional courses, but computer science courses within those programs come naturally

Skipping required courses, studying computer science diligently, and spending time daily on forums like “Jingyi Forum” and “52PoP.” Combining knowledge of assembly language and C++, Ultimately, Xiao Zheng’s choice was to write code for chips, and my family didn’t interfere; they let me choose

At that time, [name redacted], with a high level of education, joined Baidu My older sister understands me; she knows I wasn’t in a state to think or research, so she told me to talk it over with my cousin Finalize the future development path; skip going home for summer vacation, stay with the mentor, work on projects, and gain experience Entered Hengan Electronic based on a still-acceptable academic record

Graduation

Here’s the key – I took courses in information retrieval, knowing how to quickly find and locate materials and issues A helpful mentor appeared at work, gave me time to learn independently, troubleshoot issues thoroughly, and introduced me to experts at the R&D center This laid the groundwork, and now Shenzhen branch believes I’m impressive – I successfully took charge of the trading channel team

Here lies a problem; my foundational knowledge of computer operating systems, algorithms, and software engineering design… Without systematic study, it’s all based on personal experience Frequently redesigning code, lacking a consistent approach to module design – after seven years, I’m starting to feel burnt out
