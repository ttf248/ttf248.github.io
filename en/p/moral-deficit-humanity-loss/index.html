When doing things, the most important thing is to act with a clear conscience. This ensures peaceful sleep and helps you discern right from wrong. If mistakes are made, do your best to rectify them; don’t hide or try to forget. Humans are easily forgetful, yet also possess good memories. Inner peace is the ultimate destination – being able to answer yourself honestly leads to a more relaxed life.

Script

I have eight characters that carry significant weight: I feel we currently have a deficit of morality, a loss of humanity. History progresses through a cycle of problems arising and being solved, repeatedly solving them thoroughly. You need patience to wait for the reshuffling. For a country like China, many things are a slow process of reshuffling, so don’t be pessimistic during change and reshuffling.

These past few days, China has been battling two typhoons – one invisible, the other visible. The invisible typhoon is the vaccine, which challenges our sense of security. The visible typhoon rarely lands in Shanghai and impacts Beijing and Tianjin; it’s unprecedented for three major cities to face such a storm together in decades. That’s aside from the point. What you need to consider now is whether you can thrive on your own. You’ve changed greatly, possessing many answers. But if the environment remains unchanged, will you be happy? I have eight words that carry significant weight: we currently have a deficit of morality and a loss of humanity – this is the greatest deficit and the biggest loss.

People expect the safety concerns surrounding the vaccine to be resolved completely, just like the milk powder scandal. It’s important to learn from history and understand how the establishment of the US FDA and its comprehensive legal framework are closely linked to past incidents involving unsafe milk and dairy products. The Sanlu milk powder incident forced significant changes in China’s dairy industry. While I can’t guarantee a continuous series of events, I hope this one brings things to a halt. Progress occurs through cycles of problems arising, being solved, and then thoroughly resolved – otherwise, what’s the point of journalists? What’s the point of citizens?

So I think the only thing each of us can do is to pay attention. But the problem is that Chinese people tend to forget easily. As I mentioned earlier, after hitting someone’s car, we just abandon it on the side of the road and run off, with no one stopping us. A large proportion of our neighbors and colleagues are like this. So change needs to be gradual. And as ordinary citizens, what we can do is pay attention and not forget. It’s not a lack of process; when people aren’t able to eat or dress properly, talking about ideals lacks persuasiveness. But once they have enough food and warm clothes, and become the world’s leading diabetes nation, the leading high blood pressure nation, and so on – many “number one” nations – you see Chinese people start running, Chinese people start dieting. Ask the ladies here, how many of you haven’t experienced the harsh reality that you don’t have the energy to diet unless you eat a good meal? That’s a small shift.

It’s the same with the spiritual realm. When you’re well-fed and clothed, and start exercising or dieting, your spiritual needs gradually increase. For example, I used to smoke, but after starting to run, I suddenly realized I hadn’t had a cigarette in over 20 days – and haven’t since. Of course, I wouldn’t say I completely quit; that feels too formal. There might be two or three cigarettes a year. When your lifestyle changes, many things shift along with it. For Chinese people, you need patience to wait for this reshuffling.

More and more people feel unhappy, yet are unwilling to indulge in that unhappiness. While depression is on the rise, isn’t it also true that increasingly more people are seeking positive ways to live? This leads to a growth in spiritual matters. So don’t be pessimistic; it all depends on your perspective. I often see drivers cutting in line and feel frustrated. But then I quickly become optimistic because the queue on the other side is much longer now. It’s just like that – a process. Look at everyone here, willing to come to this place on a rainy evening to chat about such trivial things; isn’t that wonderful?

Isn’t this also a transformation? It means many things require a shift in thinking. I believe there will be much more to come, for example, you mentioned entrepreneurship – isn’t everyone an entrepreneur throughout their life? We attribute too much of a modern feel to the term “entrepreneurship,” but it has always existed. Su Shi was an entrepreneur; Li Bai was an entrepreneur. Throughout their lives, they created, and most didn’t succeed in creating a brand, which is fine. As long as you live with flavor and believe it’s worthwhile. What China particularly lacks now is a value system that recognizes good failure as another form of success. Chinese culture only accepts successful outcomes; it doesn’t accept a “good” failure.

I think that’s very difficult. There are many reasons why Chinese football doesn’t play well, and one of them is the fear of losing possession – no one wants to pass the ball to someone else, even if it means keeping it themselves. This is just a small reason though. I feel that after reaching my 30s, I wrote about patience and joy, but now at 50, there’s a bit of urgency because time passes so quickly, and many things you expect haven’t become reality. However, I also understand that for a country like China, many things are a slow process of change. You see your parents – some were oblivious to traffic lights, but occasionally you see a child pulling their father along. Things are changing, shuffling around, so you need to have patience.
