The evolution of trade war tariffs in 2025 presented a “escalation – counter-repressive measures – negotiations” cycle, with the US-China rivalry shifting from tariff confrontation to competition over rules. Despite short-term easing that alleviated market pressure, long-term uncertainty persists, and it’s crucial to monitor WTO rulings, supply chain adjustments, and geopolitical shifts’ ongoing impact on the global economy.

People don’t earn money beyond their own perception; the stock market crash triggered by the launch of the trade war this year has largely recovered, but countless small investors have been buried along the way.

2025 Trade War Tariff Timeline Overview

Here is a timeline of the trade war tariff events, combining key events involving the US and China, and other countries, to present the dynamic evolution of tariff policies:

One: April 2025 – Comprehensive Tariff Upgrade and Countermeasures

  1. U.S. Tariffs on China
    • April 2nd: The Trump administration signed Executive Order No. 14257, announcing the imposition of a 34% “retaliatory tariff” on goods shipped from China to the U.S., covering products originating from Hong Kong and Macau.
    • April 8th: The United States further increased the “retaliatory tariff” rate on Chinese goods from 34% to 84%, signing Executive Orders No. 14259 and 14266 to impose tariffs on additional products.
    • April 10th: The White House amended the executive order, increasing the total tariff amount on Chinese goods to 145% and raising the rate on small packages (such as goods priced below $800 from 90% to 120%).
  • China’s Countermeasures
    • April 4th: China announced a 34% tariff on all goods originating in the United States, covering sectors such as agricultural products, automobiles, and energy.
    • April 10th: China raised the retaliatory tariff rate from 34% to 84%, synchronizing with the U.S. side’s escalation.
    • April 12th: China cancelled the 91% tariffs imposed on US goods (corresponding to some of the tariffs cancelled by the US side) and suspended the implementation of the 24% tariff for 90 days, retaining a 10%.
  • Other Countries’ Tariff Game
    • U.S.-Canada Trade War: The United States imposed 25% tariffs on Canadian steel and aluminum products, and Canada retaliated by imposing taxes on US goods worth $2.98 billion CAD, and filed a WTO dispute.
    • EU and Japan: The EU imposed maximum 25% tariffs on U.S. goods, while Japan expressed concern about auto tariffs on the United States, calling the trade war “harming both sides.”

II. May 2025: Geneva Talks and Tariff De-escalation

  1. US-China High-Level Trade Talks
    • May 10-11: The US and China held a 16-hour meeting in Geneva, Switzerland, reaching the “Joint Statement on the US-China Economic Talks.”
    • May 12: Both sides announced a suspension of some tariffs:
      • The United States suspended its 24% tariff on Chinese goods (90 days), retaining 10%, and rescinded the additional tariffs imposed on April 8th and 9th.
      • China simultaneously suspended its 24% retaliatory tariffs, retaining 10%, and lifted non-tariff measures.
    • Establishment of a Consultation Mechanism: Both sides agreed to hold regular trade consultations in China, the United States, or a third country, focusing on tariff reduction and rulemaking.
  • Economic Impact & Policy Adjustments
    • US Domestic Pressure: High tariffs led to the closure of small and medium-sized enterprises (such as Amazon sellers forced to close due to tariff surges) and fueled inflationary expectations (core PCE is expected to rise to 3.3%).
    • China Rare Earth Countermeasures: China strengthened controls on rare earth exports, cracked down on smuggling, impacting US defense and new energy industries, which were granted exemptions on tariffs for key minerals such as rare earths.

III. Other Key Events and Long-Term Impacts

  1. WTO and Multilateral Reactions

    • Canada and China both lodged WTO disputes against the U.S. steel and aluminum tariffs and “retaliatory tariffs,” alleging violations of non-discrimination principles.
    • WTO Director-General Azevedo warned that U.S. tariffs could cause a 1% contraction in global trade volumes by 2025, exacerbating difficulties for developing countries.

  2. Exemption Lists and Industry Impacts

    • U.S. Excluded Goods: A 37-page list covering strategic materials such as rare earths, graphite, and medical CT balls exposed supply chain vulnerabilities.
    • Auto Tariffs: The U.S. imposed 25% tariffs on imported automobiles, leading to an estimated 2% decline in global auto production and a 9% reduction in North American output.

  3. Political and Strategic Maneuvering

    • The Trump administration attempted to reshape the international trade order through “retaliatory tariffs,” but was criticized as unilateral bullying, triggering backlash from allies (such as the EU and Japan).
    • China responded to protectionism with efforts to expand market access (such as the Cross-Border e-Commerce Expo and zero-tariff commitments), attracting cooperation from developing countries.

Five. Trends and Challenges

  • Short-Term Relief and Long-Term Competition: The suspension of U.S.-China tariffs sent positive signals, but structural issues (such as high technology and energy) remain, with midterm elections or policy continuity impacting the medium term.
  • Global Supply Chain Restructuring: Companies are accelerating adjustments to their supply chains, with Mexico and Southeast Asia benefiting from “nearshoring,” but rising costs could dampen recovery.
  • Multilateral Mechanism Dilemmas: The WTO dispute resolution mechanism is limited, regional trade agreements (such as the U.S.-UK agreement) are emerging as alternative options, but this exacerbates fragmentation.
