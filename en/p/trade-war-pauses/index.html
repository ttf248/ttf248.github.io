The evolution of trade war tariffs in 2025 presented a “escalation – counter-repressive measures – negotiations” cycle, with the US-China rivalry shifting from tariff confrontation to competition over rules. Despite short-term easing that alleviated market pressure, long-term uncertainty remains, and it’s crucial to monitor WTO rulings, supply chain adjustments, and geopolitical shifts’ sustained impact on the global economy.

2025 Trade War Tariff Timeline Overview

Here is a timeline of the trade war tariff events, combining key events involving the US and China, and other countries, to present the dynamic evolution of tariff policies:

I. April 2025: Comprehensive Upgrade and Countermeasures on Tariffs

  1. U.S. Tariff Measures Against China
    • April 2nd: The Trump administration signed Executive Order No. 14257, announcing the imposition of a 34% “retaliatory tariff” on goods exported from China to the U.S., covering products originating in Hong Kong and Macao.
    • April 8th: The United States further increased the “retaliatory tariff” rate on Chinese goods from 34% to 84%, and signed Executive Orders No. 14259 and 14266, imposing tariffs on additional products.
    • April 10th: The White House amended the executive order, increasing the total tariff amount against China to 145%, and raising the tariff rate on small packages (such as goods priced below $800 from 90% to 120%).
  • China’s Countermeasures
    • April 4th: China announced the imposition of a 34% tariff on all goods originating in the United States, covering sectors such as agricultural products, automobiles, and energy. - April 10th: China will retaliate against the tariff rate increase from 34% to 84%, in sync with the US side.
    • April 12th: China cancels the additional tariffs imposed on U.S. goods by 91% (corresponding to some of the cancellation by the US side) and suspends the implementation of 24% tariffs for 90 days, retaining 10%.

I. April 2025: Comprehensive Tariff Upgrade and Countermeasures

  • Tariff Games Involving Other Countries
    • US-Canada Trade War: The United States imposes 25% tariffs on Canadian steel and aluminum products, and Canada retaliates by taxing goods worth $298 billion from the US, as well as filing a WTO dispute.
    • EU & Japan: The EU imposes maximum 25% tariffs on US goods, while Japan expresses concern over auto tariffs imposed by the US, calling the trade war “harming both sides.”

II. May 2025: Geneva Talks and Tariff De-escalation

  1. US-China High-Level Trade Talks
    • May 10-11: The US and China held a 16-hour meeting in Geneva, Switzerland, reaching the “Joint Statement on the US-China Economic Dialogue” (also known as the Geneva Accord).
    • May 12: Both sides announced a suspension of some tariffs:
      • The United States paused its 24% tariff on Chinese goods (for 90 days), retaining 10%, and rescinded the additional tariffs imposed on April 8th and 9th.
      • China simultaneously suspended its 24% retaliatory tariffs, retaining 10%, and lifted non-tariff measures.
    • Establishment of a Consultation Mechanism: Both sides agreed to hold regular trade consultations in China, the United States, or a third country, focusing on tariff reduction and rulemaking.

III. Other Key Events and Long-Term Impacts

  1. WTO and Multilateral Responses

    • Canada and China both lodged WTO disputes against the U.S. steel and aluminum tariffs and “retaliatory tariffs,” alleging violations of non-discrimination principles.
    • WTO Director-General Azevedo warned that U.S. tariffs could cause a 1% contraction in global trade volumes by 2025, exacerbating difficulties for developing countries.

  2. Exemption Lists and Industry Impacts

    • U.S. Excluded Goods: A 37-page list covering strategic materials such as rare earths, graphite, and medical CT balls exposed supply chain vulnerabilities.
    • Auto Tariffs: The U.S. imposed 25% tariffs on imported automobiles, leading to an estimated 2% decline in global auto production and a 9% reduction in North American output.

  3. Political and Strategic Maneuvering

    • The Trump administration attempted to reshape the international trade order through “retaliatory tariffs,” but was criticized as unilateral bullying, triggering backlash from allies (such as the EU and Japan).

Five. Trends and Challenges

  • Short-Term Relief and Long-Term Competition: The suspension of US-China tariffs sent positive signals, but structural issues (such as high technology and energy) remain, with midterm elections or policy continuity impacting the medium term.
  • Global Supply Chain Restructuring: Companies are accelerating adjustments to their supply chains, with Mexico and Southeast Asia benefiting from “nearshoring,” but rising costs may dampen recovery.
  • Multilateral Mechanism Dilemmas: The WTO dispute resolution mechanism is limited, regional trade agreements (such as the US-UK agreement) are emerging as alternative options, but this could exacerbate fragmentation.
