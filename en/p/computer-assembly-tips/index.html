Destiny, beyond words

Ever since I was little, I wanted to build my own computer, but the economic conditions just wouldn’t allow it. It wasn’t until college that I finally had the chance, and for portability reasons, I got a laptop. If you want a specific time frame, when did I start wanting to build a computer? It all started at the library back home. It was a pretty decent-sized library for a city district – it had an electronic reading room (though I never went, as it charged by the hour) and a magazine reading room. That’s where I stumbled upon magazines like “大众软件” (Popular Software) and “电脑报” (Computer News). For someone with limited exposure to computers, they were essentially divine educational resources. Seeing chapters about raiding dungeons made me want to build my own computer so I could pull mobs and be a main damage dealer; seeing the “black technology” sparked fantasies of recreating what was described in the books (a discussion on using hacking tools). Though high school coursework was demanding, with my limited knowledge at the time, I felt I needed to both study and play. It was a pretty carefree time. I’d often use going to the library as an excuse, grabbing a small bag and strolling over there. The city wasn’t that big, so I usually walked. Once inside, I’d enjoy the air conditioning while reading novels, comics, and gaming magazines, occasionally glancing at some serious books.

As you get older, it’s easy to forget things. This isn’t the first time something like this has happened at the library. When I was in junior high school, my relatives assembled a computer, and I don’t remember why they built that machine back then. The operating system was Windows 2003, and the games included the card game and Age of Empires. I spent all sorts of time trying to sneak the keys and play games with my cousin.

Entering junior high, the school offered basic computer training. Later, when I transferred schools, I was also introduced to the concept of computer competitions. By the time I reached high school, I participated in NOIP once. Speaking of this, it’s impossible not to mention the power of alumni – the high school’s computer building was funded by alumni donations and included a computer lab and library. It was actually during the initial wave of China’s internet boom at that time. School leaders also supported participation in computer competitions; after all, several seniors from the previous two years had been admitted to key universities through computer-related programs.

I’ve never looked back at my relationship with computers like this before. No wonder I made such a complete shift from the automation major to the computer industry after graduation – the seeds were already sown, though those involved didn’t realize it. Having been exposed to them since childhood, I thought I was quite knowledgeable, but in reality, I only understood the basics. My biggest advantage was that initial passion.

SN550 VS SN750

The difference between the SN550 1TB and the SN750 1TB is that their sustained read/write speeds differ by a factor of two – one reaches 850MB, the other 1.6GB. However, there’s no noticeable difference in daily use because both have the same 4K performance. Of course, this refers to the 1TB capacity SN550; the 500GB and 250GB versions have slower sequential read/write speeds. Honestly, unless you’re not worried about cost, the SN550 is fine for everyday use. The main reason I didn’t buy it isn’t its sequential read/write speed, but rather that it only comes in a maximum of 1TB capacity, while the SN750 offers 2TB. For me, the value lies more in having an extra M.2 NVMe slot on my motherboard than in the price difference between these SSDs, without needing to expand further.

Choosing a Mechanical Hard Drive

Currently, mechanical hard drives are priced stably. For users with large storage needs, it is recommended to select a suitable mechanical hard drive. Frequent downloaders are advised to consider enterprise-grade hard drives, such as:

  • Western Digital Gold
  • Seagate Exos

It’s recommended to partition large-capacity mechanical hard drives. Frequent download operations should be directed to a specific partition. If bad sectors appear later, they can be concentrated in one partition, and that partition can then be discarded, effectively extending the lifespan of the mechanical hard drive.

Memory Timing Memory (Salted Fish Memory)

Memory timings (or RAM timings) are four parameters describing the performance of synchronous dynamic random-access memory (SDRAM): CL, TRCD, TRP, and TRAS, measured in clock cycles. They are typically written as four numbers separated by hyphens, such as 7-8-8-24. The fourth parameter (RAS) is often omitted, and a fifth parameter may be added: Command rate, usually 2T or 1T, also written as 2N or 1N. These parameters specify latency times that affect random access memory speed. Lower numbers generally indicate faster performance. The ultimate element determining system performance is the actual latency time, typically measured in nanoseconds.

When converting memory timings to actual delays, it’s most important to note that they are measured in clock cycles. Without knowing the duration of a clock cycle, it’s impossible to determine if one set of numbers is faster than another.

For example, the clock frequency of DDR3-2000 memory is 1000 MHz, and its clock cycle is 1 ns. Based on this 1 ns clock, a CL=7 results in an absolute latency of 7 ns. Faster DDR3-2666 (clocking at 1333 MHz with a 0.75 ns cycle) may use a larger CL=9, but still produce a shorter absolute latency of 6.75 ns.

Modern DIMMs include a serial presence detect (SPD) ROM chip, which contains memory timings recommended for automatic configuration. The PC’s BIOS may allow users to adjust these timings to improve performance (with the risk of reducing stability), or in some cases increase stability (such as using the recommended timings).

Note: Memory bandwidth measures memory throughput and is typically limited by transfer rate rather than latency. It is possible to continuously transmit at peak rates by interleaving access to multiple internal banks of SDRAM. Bandwidth can be increased at the expense of increased latency. Specifically, each new generation of DDR memory has a higher transfer rate, but absolute latency does not change significantly, especially for the first batch of products in a new generation, which often have longer latencies than the previous generation.

Even with increased memory latency, increasing memory bandwidth can improve the performance of computer systems with multiple processors or multiple execution threads. Higher bandwidth will also enhance the performance of integrated graphics cards without dedicated video memory.
